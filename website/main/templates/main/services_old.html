Zero degree experiences & training

Neutral Buoyancy Laboratory (NBL): NBL is a large pool filled with water in which astronauts practice underwater, simulating the weightlessness they will experience in space. They perform spacewalk (extravehicular activity or EVA) training in full spacesuits, using tools and equipment that mimic those on the International Space Station (ISS).


Spacecraft Simulators: These are replicas of the actual spacecraft that astronauts will use for their mission, such as the Space Shuttle or a Crew Dragon spacecraft. These simulators allow astronauts to practice launch, landing, and various in-flight procedures in a controlled environment.


Multi-Axis Space Test Inertia Facility (MASTIF): MASTIF is a centrifuge that simulates the gravitational forces astronauts experience during launch and re-entry. This helps train astronauts to withstand the G-forces involved in space travel.


Parabolic Flight Aircraft (Zero-G Plane): Astronauts often train aboard specialized aircraft like the "Vomit Comet," which flies in parabolic arcs to create brief periods of weightlessness. This allows astronauts to practice tasks and experiments in microgravity.


Robotic Arm Simulators: For missions involving the operation of robotic arms, astronauts use simulators to practice manipulating these robotic devices. This is particularly relevant for tasks such as capturing and releasing payloads, repairing satellites, or working on the ISS.


Virtual Reality (VR) Simulators: VR technology is used to simulate various aspects of space travel, including spacecraft operations, spacewalks, and emergency scenarios. It helps astronauts become familiar with spacecraft controls and procedures.


Treadmills and Exercise Equipment: Maintaining physical fitness is crucial during extended space missions. Astronauts use specialized exercise equipment, such as treadmills with harnesses to keep them grounded, to combat muscle and bone loss in the weightless environment of space.


Communication and Navigation Simulators: Astronauts practice communication with mission control and navigation procedures using simulators that mimic the spacecraft's systems.


Space Station Simulators: Simulators of the International Space Station (ISS) or other space habitats allow astronauts to become familiar with the layout, equipment, and daily routines they will encounter during their missions.


EVA Space Suits: Astronauts train with Extravehicular Mobility Units (EMUs) or space suits to practice spacewalks and other activities outside the spacecraft or space station. These suits are fully functional and provide life support during training.


High-Fidelity Simulators: These are sophisticated computer-based simulators that replicate the spacecraft's systems and environments, allowing astronauts to practice emergency procedures and mission-specific tasks.


Spacecraft Docking and Rendezvous Simulators: For missions involving spacecraft rendezvous and docking, astronauts use simulators to practice the precise manoeuvres required to approach and attach to other spacecraft or space stations



Tools for elements samples

Common types of collection devices include drills, scoops, shovels, or coring systems.

Specialized sample containers are used, which may include sealed canisters or capsules with multiple layers to maintain sample integrity.

Robotic Arms and Manipulators are often equipped with cameras and sensors to aid in sample collection and to assess the terrain.

Sampling Instruments can include spectrometers, mass spectrometers, gas analysers, or microscopes to perform preliminary analysis on the planetary surface.

Gloves and Handling Tools might include forceps, tweezers, and sample storage containers.

Sample Sealing and Packaging: Vacuum-sealed containers and thermal insulation may be used to protect the samples from temperature extremes.

Sample Return Capsule: a specialized capsule is used to protect the samples during re-entry and landing. This capsule is designed to withstand high temperatures and provide a safe environment for the samples

remote sensing instruments on spacecraft or landers are used to assess the composition, mineralogy, and topography of the planetary surface. These instruments help identify suitable sampling sites.

Real-time communication equipment is necessary to relay data and commands between the spacecraft and mission control on Earth, ensuring the success of the sample collection operation.
